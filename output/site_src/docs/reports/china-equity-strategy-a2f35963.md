Recent events have reinforced our A-share preference
Since the Iran conflict began, MSCI China has outperformed global indices by 1.4%
while the A-shares were more resilient with the CSI 300 broadly flat during this period.
We believe this is further evidence that Chinese equities offer a viable diversification
option for global investors.  Fundamentally, we see some downside protection from the
recent geopolitical events given: 1) China's exposure to oil is somewhat low at c20% of
total energy usage, 2) it has sufficient oil inventory reserves (at 4 months or 1.3bn
barrels), 3) higher oil prices are not passed through entirely to downstream customers in
China given the pricing mechanisms set by the government, and 4) higher input costs
may actually help push up PPI and pricing expectations in China, which would be a
welcome change in the current deflationary environment. Between the markets, we
prefer A-shares given: 1) downside support through potential government fund
purchases, 2) lower correlation vs global indices relative to  H-shares and ADRs, 3)
liquidity remains ample, 4) beneficiary of government policy support. Within A-share,
our sector preferences include hardware tech, non-ferrous metals, internet,  electrical
equipment, brokers and going abroad stocks.

Could a lower equity risk premium be warranted?
Historically, China A-shares have one of the lowest correlations vs global indices and this
is further evidenced in the recent bout of global volatility. It would seem therefore that
there is some diversification benefit in holding Chinese equities (particularly the A-
shares) and we believe this is not entirely reflected in the valuation with the equity risk
premium for A-shares still significantly higher vs global indices (see Figure 5).
Furthermore, we expect the following idiosyncratic factors to support the A-share
market: 1) elevated trading volume - historically there is a high correlation between
market performance and trading volume on the A-share (see Figure 6), 2) supportive
policies announced during the NPC including improving SOE returns, encouraging long-
term capital inflows and lifting company payouts and buybacks, and 3) potential retail
inflows from depositors as evidenced by the continued slowdown in household deposit
growth (see Figure 7).

Higher input costs may not be such a bad thing
While a higher input cost may put pressure on company margins, for Chinese companies
this may be less negative particularly relative to other regions. Historically, PPI has been
highly correlated with company revenue and hence as PPI rises (or becomes less
negative), this should see company revenue growth accelerate and therefore create a
positive leverage on company earnings. Furthermore, companies have been looking to
raise prices across a number of sectors and the higher oil prices may provide more
impetus for these companies (eg, in the chemical sector) to raise prices. If investors
believe the Chinese economy can  sustainably enter an inflationary environment, there is
likely to be upside to the share prices, similar to the experience in Japan (see our note).
The key factors to watch out for would be PPI as well as salaries - it would seem that
listed company wages have recovered in recent periods as shown in Figure 8.

Key findings from the 2022 UBS analysts survey on higher input costs
We conducted an analyst survey in 2022 on the potential impacts of higher energy and
shipping costs on listed company earnings (see here). Our key conclusions from the
survey were: 1) it takes 3 months on average for rising input costs to impact company
margins given inventory etc; 2) commodity and shipping costs make up c27% of total
costs on average; 3) companies can pass through 55% of the cost inflation to their
customers; 4) each 5% rise in global commodity prices and shipping costs reduces
company earnings by 7% (if not passed through). We note  the sectors whose earnings
were most insulated from higher oil prices included tech hardware and renewables. That
said, they may not always perform well (eg, tech) as in a risk-off event, but the higher
beta and well-held names generally performed worse.

Sector preferences

equities, we continue to like the large internet companies and we add JD.com and

shareholder return initiatives.
